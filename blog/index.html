Not a neutral overview. Every typeface covered here gets an opinion, a history, and an argument. We're not here to list — we're here to take a position.

No. 001
Garamond
Aa Bb Cc · 1530
SerifOld Style1530
500 years old. Still everywhere.
Claude Garamond cut these letters for the French king. They became the most copied typeface in history. 18+ versions exist — none are exact. The most common one (ITC Garamond) is considered a distortion. Adobe Garamond Pro and EB Garamond are the ones that got it right.

No. 002
Comic Sans
Aa Bb Cc · 1994
Casual1994Controversial
The most hated font in the world. Let's talk about why that's wrong.
Vincent Connare designed it in a weekend — because he was annoyed that a cartoon dog in Microsoft Bob was speaking in Times New Roman. It was never meant to leave that context. NASA used it for the Curiosity Mars rover announcement anyway. Make of that what you will.

No. 003
Baskerville
Aa Bb Cc · 1757
SerifTransitional1757
The font that makes you believe things.
John Baskerville invented his own ink, redesigned the printing press, and redesigned the paper — all to serve this typeface. In 2012, filmmaker Errol Morris ran an experiment in the New York Times: the same statement rated more true when set in Baskerville than any other font tested. He died thinking he'd failed.

typeface tested

No. 004
Papyrus
Aa Bb Cc · 1982
Display1982Font on trial
Guilty. With a footnote.
Chris Costello drew it by hand over six months — on actual papyrus. Genuinely impressive craft. Then Microsoft bundled it into Office in 1996. Then James Cameron used it for Avatar in 2009. Ryan Gosling never recovered. Neither did we. The font is not the problem. The choices were.

No. 005
FUTURA
Aa Bb Cc · 1927
Geometric Sans1927Bauhaus
The font that went to the moon.
Literally. The plaque left on the lunar surface by Apollo 11 is set in Futura. Paul Renner designed it in 1927 as pure geometry — circles, triangles, lines. No calligraphic history, no human hand, just math. NASA, IKEA, Volkswagen, Louis Vuitton. Still running the world.

No. 006
Caslon
Aa Bb Cc · 1722
SerifOld Style1722
The font that founded a nation.
The U.S. Declaration of Independence was set in Caslon. So was the Constitution. William Caslon started by cutting letters into gun barrels — and produced a typeface so good that everyone switched to it immediately. No other typeface has a saying. "When in doubt, use Caslon." That's a 300-year track record of working in almost every context.

No. 007
Akzidenz
Aa Bb Cc · 1896
Sans-serifGrotesque1896
Before Helvetica. Before all of it.
Released in 1896 by the H. Berthold type foundry in Berlin, Akzidenz-Grotesk is the quiet original — the one all those Swiss modernists were borrowing from when they claimed to be inventing something new. Helvetica didn't appear until 1957. That's 61 years of Akzidenz doing the work first.

No. 008
Helvetica
Aa Bb Cc · 1957
Neo-Grotesque1957Miedinger
The most famous font in the world. For better and for worse.
It got a documentary. It's on the New York subway, the American Apparel logo, and approximately every corporate rebrand since 1970. Max Miedinger designed it to mean nothing — to be neutral, invisible, universal. The irony is it's now the most recognizable typeface on earth. Invisibility failed spectacularly.

No. 009
Optima
Aa Bb Cc · 1958
Humanist Sans1958Zapf
The typeface that refuses to pick a side.
Hermann Zapf sketched the letterforms on gravestones in Florence in 1950. Optima has no serifs — but its strokes flare at the terminals like a classical Roman inscription. It is technically a sans-serif that behaves like a serif. The Vietnam Veterans Memorial is set in it. So is the Estée Lauder logo. Make of that what you will.
